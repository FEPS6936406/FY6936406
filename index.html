Lotus 79: Ground Effect (1978)

F1 cars of the 1970s and early 1980s had distinctive aerodynamics and
innovations. One car that stood above the rest of the competition was the
Lotus 79, which featured this technology known as ground effect. While its
predecessor, the Lotus 78 was the first car to feature this technology,
the Lotus 79 proved to be more successful, propelling Mario Andretti to the
1978 world championship.

Hughes, Piola (2020, para 3-6) states that the central tub was relatively narrow,
with two large sidepods mounted on both sides. Unlike most sidepods of the
time, which only accommodated radiators, these sidepods also housed two
venturi tunnels (a tunnel that widens), each surrounded by a carbon honeycomb. The tunnel channels
airflow from the front of the car to the rear, accelerating the air to high
speeds. This creates areas of low pressure towards the rear, generating downforce.
Hughes, Sommerfield (2023, para 3) states that another advantage
of Ground Effect was that the tunnels were placed next to the floor of the chassis,
eliminating drag which would be caused by the rear wing.

This design proved to be extremely successful, with Mario Andretti clinching
the 1978 Championship. The FIA banned ground effect in 1983 after the fatal
crashes of Ronnie Patterson in 1978 and Gilles Villeneuve in 1982.

   

Williams FW14B: Active Suspension (1992)

The FW14B, which is known as F1’s most technologically advanced cars,
which powered Nigel Mansell to the 1992 World Championship. Campbell, (2025, para 1)
stated that car had many innovations, such as traction control,
anti-lock brakes and a powerful V10 engine. However, its
groundbreaking active suspension was the main factor
for the car’s dominance.

The system used actuators instead of springs, programmed to adjust the
height of the car, to deliver understeer (when the car does not turn enough)
or oversteer (when the car’s rear end turns more than the front). This allows
the car to quickly accelerate and carry its speed through the corners. There
were also sensors that detected changes in speed, which prompted the system
to constantly adjust its ride height.

While active suspension did provide unrivalled pace in races, it added
complexity and felt unnatural to drive during the development
of the car. Despite its championship level success in 1992
and later in 1993 on the FW15C, the system was banned in 1994, which
aimed to reduce the amount of driver assists, which would be catastrophic at
speeds of 200mph if the system fails.

   

Brawn GP: Double Diffuser (2009)

The 2009 Formula 1 season will be known for a shock performance of a
new financially struggling F1 team, known as Brawn GP, who went on to win
both the drivers’ championship with Jenson Button and the Constructors
Championship.

The 2009 F1 regulations introduced major aerodynamic tweaks, resulting in
cars being a lot slower but more competitive. Ross Brawn, the team owner and
senior engineer, found a clever loophole in the diffuser size by introducing an
aerodynamic device called the Double Diffuser. This principle allowed air to channel
underneath the floor of the car and onto the rear diffuser, creating downforce. Unlike
most rear diffusers, the diffuser had a second upper channel at the rear, increasing
the volume of air exiting behind the diffuser. This allowed the car to have a significant
downforce advantage compared to rival teams. A few other teams such as Williams
and Toyota did implement a similar concept but could not achieve the same level of success.

Rival teams such as McLaren and Ferrari were not too impressed by Brawn GP’s dominance,
claiming that the double diffuser was too advantageous,
questioning the fairness of the design, going into protest with
the FIA. Although not banned directly by the
FIA, all F1 teams agreed not to use the double diffuser for
the 2011 season.

   

Mercedes W11: Dual-axis steering (DAS) (2020)

Mercedes was a dominant force in F1 and had stood above the competition
since the introduction of the turbo-hybrid engines since 2014
and have dominated for many years.

While the main factor of Mercedes’ dominance was their powerful yet
reliable engines, according to Scarborough, C (2021), DAS was a
feature that significantly helped its dominance. Introduced in 2020,
DAS was a key feature which propelled Lewis Hamilton to his record
equalling 7th world championship, with Michael Schumacher.

Normal F1 steering works like road cars, in the way that the steering
column is attached to the steering rack via pinion gears. However, DAS
adds another dimension to this system, by using a twin rack and pinion
gear system, allowing the front wheels to turn independently to each other.
This allows the angle of the front wheels to to be changed, which is known
as toe.

On the straights, there needs to be zero toe on the wheels, as this
minimises drag, so the Mercedes drivers could pull back on the steering
wheel, to perfectly align the front wheels. During braking, the driver
can push back on the steering wheel, which changes the angle of the front
wheels to toe-out, allowing smooth entry into a corner. While exiting a
corner, the driver can pull back on the steering wheel to induce toe-in,
allowing the outside tire to turn more, increasing grip and resulting in
faster exit from corners. DAS also helped to maintain consistent tire grip
during races.

The FIA initially deemed this system legal but protests from rival teams
about the fairness and advantage of the system resulted in the FIA banning
DAS for the 2021 season onwards.
