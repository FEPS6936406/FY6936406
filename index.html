Lotus 79 Ground Effect, 1978

  

  
F1 cars of the 1970s and early 1980s had distinctive aerodynamics and 
innovations. One car that stood above the rest of the competition was the 
Lotus 79, which featured this technology known as ground effect. While its 
predecessor, the Lotus 78 was the first car to feature ground effect, 
the Lotus 79 proved to be more successful, propelling Mario Andretti to the 
1978 world championship.

  
The central tub was relatively narrow, with two large sidepods mounted on both sides. Unlike most sidepods of 
the time, which only accommodated radiators, these sidepods also housed 
two venturi tunnels, each surrounded by a carbon honeycomb. The tunnel 
channels airflow from the front of the car to the rear, allowing air to 
accelerated to extremely high speeds, creating low pressure areas towards the rear. This results in downforce 
being generated. Another advantage of Ground Effect was that the tunnels were 
placed next to the floor of the chassis, eliminating drag which would be caused by the rear wing. 
This design proved to be extremely successful, with Mario Andretti clinching the 1978 Championship. The FIA 
banned ground effect in 1983 as a result of the cars becoming dangerously fast 
and after the fatal crashes of Ronnie Patterson in 1978 and Gilles Villeneuve in 1982.
